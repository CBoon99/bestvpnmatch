Best VPN for China in 2026 — What Actually Works

China's Great Firewall (GFW) is the most sophisticated internet censorship system in the world. It uses deep packet inspection (DPI), IP blocking, DNS poisoning, and active probing to detect and kill VPN connections. Most VPNs that work fine in Europe and the US fail completely inside China. Technical background

What the Great Firewall Actually Does

Understanding the GFW's detection mechanisms explains why only obfuscated protocols survive it — and why standard OpenVPN or WireGuard connections fail within seconds of connecting from inside China.

Technique 1

Deep Packet Inspection (DPI)

The GFW reads packet headers and payload patterns to identify VPN protocols. OpenVPN, WireGuard, and IKEv2 all have distinctive byte sequences in their handshakes that DPI can match against signatures in milliseconds. Standard VPN traffic cannot hide from DPI without an obfuscation layer.

Technique 2

IP Blocking

Known VPN server IP addresses are blacklisted at the router level. VPN providers counter this by rotating IP addresses constantly. The GFW maintains an up-to-date list of known data centre IP ranges and blocks them wholesale — which is why smaller VPNs with limited server infrastructure fail almost immediately.

Technique 3

DNS Poisoning

DNS queries for VPN-related domains are intercepted and return incorrect IP addresses. Querying expressvpn.com or nordvpn.com from inside China returns a blocked or wrong IP. This is why you can't visit VPN websites to download software once you're in-country.

Technique 4

Active Probing

When the GFW suspects a connection might be a VPN, it sends its own probing packets to the suspected server and analyses the response. This is how it catches some obfuscated connections that fool passive DPI — the server's behaviour under probing reveals it as a VPN endpoint.

Technique 5

SNI Inspection

During the TLS handshake, the Server Name Indication (SNI) field reveals the destination hostname in plaintext. The GFW can read this to identify connections to VPN provider infrastructure, even when the payload is encrypted. Encrypted Client Hello (ECH) partially mitigates this, but GFW countermeasures continue to evolve.

The only solution: obfuscated protocols. Obfuscated VPN traffic is engineered to look like ordinary HTTPS traffic — the same protocol used for every website you visit. The GFW cannot block HTTPS without breaking the entire web, so well-implemented obfuscation can survive DPI. Not all obfuscation is equal: ExpressVPN's Lightway with obfuscation and Astrill's StealthVPN are the two implementations that consistently survive the GFW's active probing in 2026.

What Doesn't Work in China

Every VPN below has been reported by users to fail inside China in 2025–2026 testing, for specific technical reasons.

  • ✕
    Free VPNs (any of them) Free VPN IP address ranges are known and catalogued by the GFW. They get blocked within days of being identified. Free providers don't have the infrastructure budget to keep rotating addresses fast enough. There are no reliable free VPNs for China.
  • ✕
    Mullvad Mullvad is an excellent privacy-focused VPN but does not offer obfuscation sufficient for the GFW. Its DAITA (Defence Against AI-guided Traffic Analysis) feature is aimed at adversarial AI analysis, not DPI-based national censorship. Mullvad connections are consistently blocked inside China.
  • ✕
    Proton VPN Proton's Stealth protocol has mixed results in China. Some users report it working briefly after a fresh connection, but it's not reliable enough to depend on. Proton VPN's Chinese infrastructure footprint is limited and the company has not made China-specific obfuscation a product priority.
  • ✕
    WireGuard (standard, non-obfuscated) WireGuard is fast and modern, but its packet headers have a completely distinctive signature that the GFW identifies immediately via DPI. Any VPN app using standard WireGuard without an obfuscation wrapper will fail in China. Note: Astrill's WireGuard implementation wraps it in obfuscation — that's a different product from standard WireGuard.
  • ✕
    Any VPN you haven't tested outside China first If you haven't confirmed your VPN connects successfully before entering China, assume it won't work when you need it. Test on your home network before you travel. The time to discover a VPN doesn't work is not in a hotel room in Chengdu.

Before you travel

Step by Step: Setting Up Before You Land

Follow every step below. Skipping any of them increases the chance you'll arrive with a VPN that either doesn't work or can't be fixed from inside China.

  1. 1
    Choose ExpressVPN or Astrill — ideally install both If your budget allows, install both. ExpressVPN is the more cost-effective single choice; Astrill is the better option if you're staying long-term or need maximum reliability for work. Having two VPNs means one works when the other has an outage.
  2. 2
    Download and install on every device you're bringing Laptop, phone, tablet — install and log in on all of them. VPN apps require an internet connection to first authenticate your account. Do this while you're outside China. If you forget a device, you won't be able to install remotely from inside the country.
  3. 3
    Connect and confirm it works before you leave Open the VPN app, connect to a server in a country you'd use from China (e.g. Japan, Singapore, or US), and verify that your traffic routes correctly. Visit whatismyip.com to confirm your IP has changed. This is your baseline test that the app and your account are working.
  4. 4
    Save customer support contact details offline Write down or screenshot the support email address and any alternative contact URLs (not the main website — those are blocked). ExpressVPN publishes a special URL for users inside China accessible via their installed app. Find it before you land and save it somewhere you can access without internet.
  5. 5
    Download manual config files as a backup ExpressVPN lets you download manual OpenVPN and Lightway configuration files from your account page. Download these to your devices before travel. If the app stops working, you may be able to connect using a manual config through the built-in VPN client on your device — bypassing the need for the app at all.
  6. 6
    On iPhone: check your App Store account region Chinese App Store accounts do not have VPN apps listed — Apple complies with Chinese regulations. If your primary Apple ID has a Chinese region set, you'll need to create or switch to a non-Chinese App Store account (US, UK, or any other supported region) to download VPN apps. Do this before you travel so you're not locked out.

Legal context

Legal Note on VPN Use in China

Using a VPN in China is technically illegal for individuals under Chinese law. The Chinese government requires VPN providers to obtain government approval; unapproved VPNs are prohibited. In practice, enforcement against tourists and foreign visitors is extremely rare — there are no documented cases of a foreign national being prosecuted solely for using a VPN during a visit. Fines have occasionally been issued to Chinese citizens. Foreigners may receive a warning in rare circumstances. The legal risk for a short-term visitor using a reputable VPN for personal use is considered very low, but it is not zero. This page provides factual information. It is not a legal recommendation, and you should make your own assessment of the risk before travel.

Which VPN protocol works in China?
Standard protocols — OpenVPN UDP/TCP, WireGuard, and IKEv2 — are detected and blocked by the GFW through deep packet inspection. The packet signatures of these protocols are well-documented and trivially identified. Protocols that work are obfuscated variants that disguise VPN traffic as normal HTTPS. ExpressVPN's Lightway with obfuscation is the most widely reported to work reliably. Astrill's StealthVPN and OpenWeb protocols were built specifically for the GFW and are the most technically sophisticated implementations available. NordVPN's obfuscated servers run OpenVPN with an obfuscation layer and can also work, but less consistently than the other two.

You must install your VPN before you arrive. If you're already inside China without a VPN installed, your options are very limited: a trusted contact outside China may be able to send you an installation file directly, or you could try accessing a provider's mirror URL via email (some providers send these to existing customers).
