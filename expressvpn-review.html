Why Lightway is the fastest VPN protocol tested

Lightway is ExpressVPN's proprietary protocol, built entirely in-house and open sourced in 2021. Unlike WireGuard (which NordLynx is built on), Lightway was designed by ExpressVPN specifically with VPN use cases in mind.

The key engineering decisions that contribute to its speed:

  • wolfSSL instead of OpenSSL — smaller codebase, faster handshake, smaller attack surface
  • UDP mode by default — optimised for throughput, lower latency than TCP
  • Sub-1-second reconnection — reconnects in under 1 second after a network change vs 3–8 seconds for WireGuard. Critical on mobile when switching between WiFi and cellular
  • Smaller codebase — fewer lines of code = easier to audit, fewer vulnerabilities

Open source: Lightway's source code is publicly available on GitHub. Anyone can inspect the implementation. This is meaningfully different from closed-source protocols used by some competitors.

Even ExpressVPN's OpenVPN implementation (310 Mbps) is faster than many VPNs' best protocols — a testament to server infrastructure quality as much as protocol design.

Privacy and security — 96/100 privacy score

Jurisdiction, audit history, and real-world evidence reviewed.

British Virgin Islands jurisdiction

ExpressVPN is incorporated in the British Virgin Islands. The BVI is outside the 5-Eyes, 9-Eyes, and 14-Eyes intelligence-sharing agreements. It has no mandatory data retention laws — companies are not required to store user activity logs. This is one of the most favourable VPN jurisdictions available.

KPMG no-logs audit

ExpressVPN's no-logs policy has been independently verified by KPMG. The audit was published in 2023 and confirmed that ExpressVPN does not store connection logs, IP addresses, browsing activity, or any information that could link a user to their activity on the VPN.

Why audits matter: Any VPN can claim a no-logs policy. Independent audits by reputable firms — KPMG in this case — provide third-party verification. Look for audits that inspect both the policy and the technical infrastructure.

TrustedServer — RAM-only architecture

TrustedServer is the most important technical privacy feature ExpressVPN offers. Every server runs entirely on RAM — no hard drives, no persistent storage of any kind. The operating system, software, and any temporary data live only in volatile memory.

When a server is rebooted — which ExpressVPN does regularly by design — all of that memory is wiped completely. There is nothing for a third party to seize or read, even if they physically take possession of a server.

PwC verified: The TrustedServer architecture has been separately verified by PwC (PricewaterhouseCoopers), confirming the RAM-only implementation functions as described.

2021 Turkish government server seizure

In 2021, Turkish authorities seized an ExpressVPN server as part of a political investigation into the assassination of the Russian ambassador. This is the most significant real-world test of ExpressVPN's no-logs policy.

Result: Turkish investigators found no useful data. The server contained no logs, no user information, and nothing that helped the investigation. This remains a strong real-world validation of TrustedServer and the no-logs policy.

Kape Technologies acquisition — transparency section

What you should know: ExpressVPN was acquired by Kape Technologies in September 2021 for approximately $936 million. Kape Technologies also owns Private Internet Access (PIA) and CyberGhost. Kape was formerly known as Crossrider, a company with a history in adware and browser extensions that were used for ad injection. This history is relevant context.

Since the acquisition, ExpressVPN has continued to operate with its own independent team and leadership. The privacy policy has not changed, and the KPMG audit (2023) was completed post-acquisition — giving it meaningful weight. The Turkish server seizure also predates and confirms the policy.

That said, ownership does matter over time. The concern is not what has happened since 2021, but what could happen in the future under corporate direction. Users who place privacy above everything should track future audit publications and any policy changes.

We score ExpressVPN 88/100 on ethics — lower than its privacy score precisely because of this ownership context. It is not a disqualifier, but it is information you deserve to have.

Lightway protocol — deep-dive

ExpressVPN's proprietary protocol, open sourced in 2021.

Lightway is not built on WireGuard — a point worth clarifying, since many newer VPN protocols are. ExpressVPN built Lightway from the ground up, making different engineering tradeoffs than WireGuard. The result is a protocol optimised specifically for VPN connections rather than general-purpose networking.

Lightway UDP

Default mode. Optimised for speed and low latency. Achieved 932 Mbps based on published benchmarks. Best for streaming, gaming, and general browsing.

Lightway TCP

Better for restricted networks. More reliable in environments with aggressive firewalls. Used in China and other censored networks.

Obfuscated Lightway

Disguises VPN traffic as standard HTTPS. Makes it very difficult for deep packet inspection (DPI) to identify and block the connection. Essential for China.

wolfSSL encryption

Uses wolfSSL instead of OpenSSL. Smaller codebase reduces attack surface. Faster TLS handshake. FIPS 140-2 validated.

Reconnection speed matters on mobile

Lightway reconnects in under 1 second after a network change — for example, switching from home WiFi to 4G while commuting. WireGuard typically takes 3–8 seconds. During that reconnection window, your true IP address can be briefly exposed if the kill switch is not active. Lightway's sub-second reconnection substantially reduces this window.

Open source: Lightway's full source code is available on GitHub (github.com/expressvpn/lightway-core). It has been independently security-audited. This transparency is unusual for a proprietary protocol and puts it in the same category as WireGuard for verifiability.
