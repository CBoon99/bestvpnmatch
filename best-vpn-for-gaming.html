School & University WiFi Bypass

Most school networks run commercial firewalls — things like Cisco Umbrella, Palo Alto, or Fortinet — that use deep packet inspection (DPI) to identify and block traffic by type. Gaming traffic gets flagged. So do standard VPN protocols like OpenVPN and IKEv2, which have recognisable handshakes that DPI catches immediately.

The solution is a VPN with obfuscation. Obfuscation wraps your VPN traffic inside what looks like ordinary HTTPS — the same protocol used for every website. The firewall sees what appears to be normal web browsing and lets it through. Your Roblox or Fortnite session is inside that encrypted tunnel.

NordVPN's obfuscated servers are the most reliable for bypassing school DPI. Enable them in Settings → Advanced → Obfuscated servers. ExpressVPN's Lightway protocol also passes DPI in most configurations — no extra settings needed.

Free VPNs fail at this because their IP ranges are publicly known. Security vendors maintain lists of VPN provider IP addresses and block them wholesale. Even if the protocol gets through, the IP won't. That's why Proton VPN free sometimes gets through (their IP pool overlaps with legitimate services) but it's hit-or-miss. For reliable school bypass, NordVPN or ExpressVPN are the practical choices.

Connection steps for school WiFi: Connect to school WiFi normally → Open NordVPN → Enable obfuscated servers → Connect to your nearest server → Launch your game. The whole thing takes about 30 seconds.

Stop DDoS Attacks

When you connect to most online games, your real IP address gets exposed — either to game servers that log it, or directly to other players in peer-to-peer games like some Call of Duty modes, older Minecraft setups, and various fighting games. If someone with your IP wants to knock you offline, they can send a packet flood to your connection and you're done for the match.

This isn't theoretical. In competitive gaming communities — esports, ranked play, high-stakes lobbies — this happens regularly. Tournament players, streamers, and anyone who's made enemies in a game are common targets.

A VPN masks your real IP. When you're connected, game servers and other players see the VPN server's IP, not yours. Even if someone gets that VPN IP and floods it, they're targeting a server with enterprise-grade infrastructure — not your home router. NordVPN and ExpressVPN both route through data centres built to absorb this kind of traffic.

For maximum DDoS protection: choose a VPN server geographically close to the game server you're joining, not just the one closest to you. This minimises the ping hit while keeping your real IP masked.

Mullvad is also worth mentioning here — their no-logs policy is independently audited, and they accept cash payments, which means there's genuinely no account trail. If privacy is your primary concern rather than school bypass, Mullvad at €5/month flat is a solid choice.

Will a VPN increase or decrease my ping?

It depends. With a quality VPN on a server near the game server, the added latency is typically 5–15ms — noticeable in benchmarks but undetectable in actual gameplay for most genres. If you're playing on a local server, VPN overhead is minimal.

A VPN can actually reduce ping if your ISP is traffic-shaping or throttling gaming traffic — a practice more common than ISPs admit. By routing inside an encrypted tunnel, the ISP can't identify the traffic as gaming and throttle it. ExpressVPN's Lightway protocol has the lowest overhead of any VPN we evaluated.
